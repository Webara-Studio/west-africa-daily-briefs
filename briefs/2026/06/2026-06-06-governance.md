# 🌍 Governance, Elections, Regulation & Trade — Saturday, 6 June 2026

## Headline Trends

West Africa's governance landscape this weekend is dominated by **Nigeria's deepening security crisis and its inextricable link to the 2027 election**, **Ghana's escalating anti-LGBTQ+ bill controversy**, and **continental divisions over the African Family Values Charter**. The kidnapping of schoolchildren in Oyo State has moved from a security story to a full-blown governance crisis, with families still waiting after 20+ days. Meanwhile, in Ghana, the internal NDC rift over the LGBTQ bill reveals the Mahama government's balancing act between its parliamentary majority, religious constituents, and international stakeholders. The adoption of the African Family Values Charter in Accra — with South Africa and Mozambique abstaining — underscores the deepening divide between constitutionally progressive and socially conservative African states.

## Sentiment Snapshot

Sentiment is **mixed, leaning negative**. Nigeria's trajectory is the dominant concern — the kidnapping crisis expanding southward, state governors issuing emergency executive orders, and stakeholders openly blaming the government's lack of planning for governance failure. The 2027 election is no longer a distant prospect; it is actively shaping political behaviour, with Tinubu's re-election machinery already mobilising. Ghana's political environment is more stable but increasingly polarised on social issues. The African Family Values Charter vote reveals a continent pulling in two directions simultaneously. For businesses and investors, the governance risk premium on West Africa is rising — but so is the clarity of the political landscape, which creates its own opportunities for those who can read the terrain.

## Deep Dive

### 1. Elections & Political Developments

**Nigeria — 2027 Campaign Takes Shape**

The 2027 general election — now roughly 19 months away — is already actively shaping political behaviour on both sides of the aisle:

- **Tinubu re-election mobilisation**: The Christian Youth Movement (CYM) has publicly declared its mobilisation for the Tinubu-Shettima re-election ticket. This is significant because Christian voter turnout in the North-Central and South-West will be decisive. The Premium Times interview with CYM leadership reveals an organised, faith-based electoral machine activating well ahead of formal campaign season.

- **Opposition fragmentation deepens**: The Appeal Court voided the recognition of a PDP factional caretaker committee, further weakening the main opposition party's institutional capacity. This follows the Fayose controversy in Oyo, where the former governor was accused of politicising the abduction crisis. The PDP's internal dysfunction continues to benefit the ruling APC.

- **Security as the election's defining issue**: The Reuters analysis from Oyo State is unequivocal — kidnapping and insecurity will be the primary criteria on which Nigerians judge politicians in 2027. SBM Intelligence's Cheta Nwanze put it plainly: "As the 2027 elections approach, Nigerians will judge politicians primarily on whether they can keep classrooms and communities safe."

- **Governor Makinde's emergency response**: Oyo State Governor Seyi Makinde signed Executive Order No. 002 of 2026, restricting commercial motorcycle (okada) operations to between 5:30 a.m. and 10:30 p.m., enforcing vehicle registration, and empowering authorities to arrest offenders and impound unregistered vehicles. This is a direct response to the Oriire school abductions and reflects the growing trend of subnational executive action to compensate for federal security failures.

**African Family Values Charter — Continental Divide**

The 4th Inter-Parliamentary Conference in Accra became a flashpoint for continental social governance:

- **South Africa abstained**, with delegation head Zandile Majozi stating the Charter's definition of marriage as exclusively between a man and a woman conflicts with the South African Constitution and international legal principles.
- **Mozambique also abstained**, citing logistical and legislative scheduling constraints — though this may mask similar constitutional reservations.
- The Charter seeks to promote family values, sovereignty, and cultural norms across the continent. Its adoption — even with abstentions — signals a growing effort by socially conservative parliamentary blocs to codify traditional values at the continental level.
- For businesses, this creates a bifurcated regulatory environment: companies operating across multiple African markets will need to navigate increasingly divergent social legislation on gender, family law, and civil rights.

### 2. Regulatory Changes

**Ghana — Anti-LGBTQ+ Bill Turmoil**

Damongo MP Samuel Abu Jinapor has escalated his challenge to the amended anti-LGBTQ+ bill, accusing the Mahama government of diluting the original 2024 legislation:

- Jinapor claims Parliament approved **31 amendments** to the bill before passing the revised version on May 29, 2026 — and that the amendments "substantially altered the character of the bill and reduced its effectiveness."
- The most contentious changes include exemptions for government institutions, NGOs, development partners, academic institutions, and health-related programmes — which Jinapor argues create loopholes for LGBTQ advocacy under the umbrella of public health.
- He is calling on the government to assent to the **original 2024 bill** without modification, or reintroduce it verbatim.
- This creates a difficult position for President Mahama: assent to the original bill risks international donor and ESG investor backlash; assent to the amended version risks alienating the religious and traditional base; further delay risks appearing indecisive.
- **Commercial implication**: International businesses operating in Ghana should monitor this closely. Assent to either version will have implications for corporate diversity policies, employee protections, and investor relations. The amended version's health-related exemptions could create compliance uncertainty for health sector companies and NGOs.

**Nigeria — Governance Failure Diagnosis**

Stakeholders are becoming more blunt in their assessment of Nigeria's governance challenges:

- A Guardian Nigeria report quotes stakeholders directly blaming **lack of planning** as the root cause of Nigeria's governance failure. This is not a partisan critique — it reflects a broad consensus that Nigeria's governance deficits are structural, not merely political.
- The **pharmaceutical manufacturers group (PMG-MAN) has targeted 70% local drug production** — a significant policy ambition that, if implemented, would reshape Nigeria's pharmaceutical supply chain and create openings for generic drug manufacturers and supply chain businesses.
- The **SSDC has warned businesses against cyber and election-related risks** ahead of 2027 — signalling that the cybersecurity regulatory environment is tightening in parallel with the political cycle.
- **Engineers are urging the government to prioritise diesel price relief for productive sectors** — a reminder that energy costs remain a critical constraint on Nigerian manufacturing and industrial output.

**Nigeria — Ogoni Cleanup & Environmental Governance**

On World Environment Day, HYPREP (the Hydrocarbon Pollution Remediation Project) reported that the Ogoni cleanup is sequestering carbon and reviving livelihoods. This is a rare positive story in Nigeria's environmental governance and could serve as a model for broader Niger Delta remediation — though progress remains slow and contested.

### 3. Security & Stability

**Nigeria — Kidnapping Nightmare Spreads South**

The defining security story of the week is the dramatic geographic expansion of Nigeria's kidnapping crisis:

- **Oyo State (Southwest)**: The May 15 attacks on three schools in the Oriire district — kidnapping 30+ students and teachers, killing one teacher — shattered the perception that the southwest is safe from the banditry and kidnapping that has plagued the north. Families are still waiting, 20+ days later. A video allegedly showing the beheading of a seized teacher is circulating (unverified by Reuters).
- **Ondo State**: Gunmen attacked a community and abducted a pastor's 9-year-old child — further evidence of the southward spread.
- **Northwest Nigeria**: Gunmen abducted seven students, according to police.
- **Oyo_okada restrictions**: Governor Makinde's executive order banning nighttime okada operations is a direct operational response, though its effectiveness remains to be seen.
- **National ransom economy**: SBM Intelligence estimates kidnappers collected at least **₦2.57 billion ($1.89 million) in ransom payments** in the year to June 2025 — the true figure is likely far higher.
- **Human impact**: The farming communities in Oyo's Oriire district are fleeing, abandoning farms and livelihoods. Local chief Tajudeen Abioye told Reuters that many residents lack even mobile phone coverage to call for help.

**The Tinubu security record is now the central political question of 2027.**

President Tinubu pledged to tackle insecurity upon taking office — recruiting more soldiers and police, better equipping and paying them. But the geographic expansion of kidnapping into the southwest, combined with the perceived slow response to the Oyo crisis, has created a genuine political vulnerability. As Reuters notes: "Tinubu will seek re-election and is likely to enter the race as favourite... However, worsening security could weigh on his chances."

**Regional Security Notes:**

- **African migrants flee South Africa** as xenophobic violence surges — hundreds have fled into the mountains. This has implications for ECOWAS-SACU relations and the treatment of West African diaspora communities.
- **Afrobarometer survey**: Many Africans see justice systems as unequal, costly, and slow — a finding that signals both a governance deficit and an opportunity for legal technology and alternative dispute resolution services.

While the ECOWAS website's most recent headline news is administrative (student visits, scholarship ceremonies), the institutional groundwork laid in recent weeks — the Business Council operationalisation and the AfCFTA translation work highlighted in last week's brief — continues to move forward. The 4th Inter-Parliamentary Conference in Accra (which produced the Family Values Charter vote) demonstrates that ECOWAS and pan-African parliamentary bodies are actively convening and generating policy outputs, even if the headlines are dominated by security.

**AfCFTA — Startup Program Application Window**

The AfCFTA Startup Partnership Program 2026 remains open for applications (as noted in last week's brief). Early-stage companies in fintech, agritech, and logistics should note this continental market entry opportunity.

### 5. Commercial Angle

**The sharpest commercial read on governance in West Africa right now comes down to three calculations:**

**1. Nigeria's security crisis is a business crisis.** The kidnapping epidemic is not just a humanitarian catastrophe — it is disrupting agriculture (farming communities fleeing), education (school closures), and commerce (transport restrictions like the okada ban). Every day the crisis persists, Nigeria's economic output contracts further, and the 2027 election timeline accelerates political risk. Businesses with Nigerian exposure should be stress-testing operations against scenarios of prolonged instability, further geographic spread of kidnapping, and potential post-election policy disruption.

**2. Ghana's LGBTQ bill is an ESG and investment risk event — but also a clarity event.** Whatever Mahama decides, the decision will create regulatory clarity that businesses can plan around. The amended version with health-related exemptions is more business-friendly than the original; the original version would trigger stronger international scrutiny. Companies should prepare compliance frameworks for both scenarios.

**3. The African Family Values Charter is an early warning system.** The South Africa/Mozambique abstentions reveal that African continental governance is not monolithic — there is an increasingly pronounced split between constitutionally progressive and socially conservative states. Businesses operating across multiple African markets should map their operations against this divide and prepare for divergent regulatory environments on social policy, gender, and family law.

**The counterintuitive opportunity**: Nigeria's governance crisis is creating space for private sector solutions. Security technology, community alert systems, emergency communications, pharmaceutical localisation, and legal tech are all sectors where demand is being driven by governance failure. The companies that can deliver practical solutions in Nigeria's difficult operating environment will build durable competitive advantages.
