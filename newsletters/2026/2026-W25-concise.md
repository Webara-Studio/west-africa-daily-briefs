# West Africa Weekly — 2026 W25

**Week of June 15–21, 2026**

West Africa's commercial landscape this week was defined by regulatory sharp turns, a quiet energy revolution, and currency dislocations creating new windows for local manufacturers. From Accra's crypto crackdown to Abidjan's solar ambitions and Lagos's import substitution imperative, the region is entering a phase where first-movers with compliant, locally rooted models stand to capture outsized value.

---

## 1. Bank of Ghana Draws a Hard Line on Crypto — Just as the Sandbox Opens

The Bank of Ghana ordered all financial institutions to immediately sever ties with unauthorised crypto platforms offering fiat wallet services — a directive issued with no grace period. The move targets platforms facilitating cedi deposits and withdrawals without regulatory approval. In parallel, Ghana's Securities and Exchange Commission advanced its VASP sandbox, with 11 companies (including Africoin, Goldbod, and Vaulta) potentially receiving full licences as early as September 2026. Sub-Saharan Africa received over $205 billion in on-chain crypto value in the year to June 2025, up 52% year-on-year, with Nigeria leading at $92 billion.

**Why it matters:** The regulatory bifurcation — central bank enforcement versus SEC sandbox progression — creates a narrow but lucrative corridor for compliant operators. The first platform to bridge mobile money and stablecoin wallets at scale within Ghana's regulatory framework will capture enormous volume. Businesses building licensed on/off-ramps for UK→Ghana and US→Nigeria remittance corridors are exceptionally well positioned, particularly given that Africa leads the world in stablecoin ownership at 79%.

## 2. Africa's Solar Boom Is Four Times Bigger Than Anyone Thought

Chinese import data reveals that actual solar deployment across Africa is nearly four times official figures, driven not by state-planned utility projects but by decentralised commercial and industrial rooftop installations. Ivory Coast is emerging as West Africa's solar leader, targeting 1,044 MW by 2030 with 149 MW already under construction and record-low IPP tariffs from the Infinity/Masdar joint venture. Solar-plus-storage microgrids are now delivering competitive baseload power, with Wood Mackenzie modelling showing hybrid PV-plus-storage consistently outperforming coal and gas on levelised cost of energy.

**Why it matters:** Ghana's commercial and industrial rooftop solar segment has massive headroom — a $200 million Swiss-backed programme targets 137 MW, yet only 66 MW is installed to date. EPC providers, financiers, and developers who move now can lock in favourable tariffs before the market matures. Agrivoltaics in northern Nigeria (Kano, Katsina, Jigawa) represent a further frontier where less than 1.5% of cropland could meet projected mid-century solar demand.

## 3. Ghana's Stock Exchange Is the Best-Performing Market You're Not Watching

The Ghana Stock Exchange Composite Index closed the week at 14,442, up an extraordinary 64.67% year-to-date and 137.85% over twelve months — despite a modest pullback from its June 17 peak of 14,640. The rally is drawing international attention, with the BRVM in Abidjan also posting gains (+0.50%) and Kenya's NSE riding a genuine investor wave with a growing IPO pipeline.

**Why it matters:** Ghana's equity rally is creating a platform for capital formation that extends well beyond the exchange itself. A Ghana-focused ETF or diaspora bond could channel international capital into the cedi-denominated surge. Nigeria's government is already considering a second diaspora bond issuance. For businesses listed on or considering the GSE, the current momentum offers a rare window to raise equity capital at favourable valuations — before global macro conditions shift.

## 4. Nigeria's Shea Nut Export Ban Creates a $300 Million Processing Opportunity

Nigeria extended its raw shea nut export ban to February 2027, effectively mandating domestic processing and creating a captive supply chain for local manufacturers. Kwara State has already commissioned a 50-tonne shea butter factory, and the federal government — backed by $13 million from USAID and the Global Shea Alliance — is targeting $300 million in annual value addition. Cocoa prices, meanwhile, have corrected sharply from late-2025 highs of $5,470/tonne to approximately $4,060/tonne, pressuring farmer incomes in Ghana and Ivory Coast.

**Why it matters:** The shea export ban is a textbook import substitution play — and it is backed by both political will and development finance. Processors who establish operations now benefit from guaranteed domestic supply, government incentives, and access to a global shea market projected to exceed $3 billion by 2028. The cocoa price correction, while painful for farmers, creates an entry point for offtakers and processors with balance sheet resilience.

## 5. Naira Devaluation Forces Nigeria's Import Substitution Window Wide Open

The naira's devaluation from ₦400/$1 to ₦1,700/$1 has made imports prohibitively expensive across Nigeria, triggering a structural shift toward local manufacturing. Multinationals are exiting or scaling back, while domestic producers in cement, building materials, tomato processing, and pharmaceuticals are stepping in to fill the gap. Ghana faces the opposite pressure — cedi appreciation is making imports cheaper and exports less competitive, with a 23% compliance burden on CIF value adding to the pain.

**Why it matters:** Nigeria's import substitution window is the most significant manufacturing opportunity in West Africa this year. Local producers who can scale quickly — particularly in fast-moving consumer goods, pharmaceuticals, and agro-processing — will capture market share that multinationals are vacating. In Ghana, the cedi's strength is a warning sign for exporters but an opportunity for import-dependent businesses and consumers. Both dynamics favour entrepreneurs building locally rooted supply chains.
